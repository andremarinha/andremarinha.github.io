excerpt: 'The cycle of contention held in the first half of the 1990s against the implementation of Law 20/92 was referred to as the last sign of the political strength of student activism in Portugal, thirty years after the academic crisis that opposed university students to the country’s authoritarian regime. After a trajectory of decline between 1992 and 2017, the Portuguese Student Movement has been able to re-mobilise and create opportunities for broader protest campaigns on behalf of exogenous factors to the Field of Higher Education. As data collected on protest events called by Basic and Secondary and Higher Education Students for the period between 2003 and 2021 illustrates, its recent activity has been defined by the extension of its discourse to reach a broader audience, articulating itself with environmental, housing, anti-racist, and anti-fascist groups. Contrasting with the 1990s last cycle of mobilisation, against the government's decision to implement tuition fees in public higher education, the contemporary (re)emergence of the Portuguese university student movement is inscribed in the general reconfiguration of the country’s discursive field, progressively permeable to colonial images and authoritarian discourses. The re-incorporation of anti-racist and anti-fascist claims into the ‘repertoires of critique’ influenced both the Portuguese Student Movement and the social movement sector, hence forging discursive continuities for movement solidarity and broad protest campaigns in an often described ‘mild-mannered’ country. Our research suggests that the reconfiguration and re-politicisation of the Portuguese discursive field followed two critical moments: the (i) decision of the Faculty of Social Sciences and Humanities of NOVA University of Lisbon Students’ Association, in March 2017, to cancel a conference on ‘populism or democracy’ organised by Nova Portugalidade (New Portugality), a ‘nationalist and colonialist group’; and the (ii) October 2019 general election, with the radical right political party Chega (Enough) securing a parliamentary seat. Based upon the triangulation of data from our Protest Event Analysis (PEA) database and from digital ethnographic research of a student platform (Brigada Estudantil), this paper explores recent solidarity dynamics between social movements in Portugal through the concept of ‘common repertoires of critique’.'
